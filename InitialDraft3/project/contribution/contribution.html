Part 1: Comandante

The commandante, Don Senor el Coronel Encarnacion Rios, who was loyal to the Ins and suspected Goodwin's devtion to the Outs, hissed: "AAha!" and wrote in his secret memorandum book the accusive fact that Senor Goodwin had on the momentuous date received a telegram.

In the midst of the hullabaloo a man stepped to the door of a small wooden building and looked out. Above the door was a sign that read "Keogh and Clancy" -a nomenclature that seemed not to be idigenous to that tropical soil. The man in the door was Billy Keogh, scout of fortune and progress and latter-day rover of the Spanish Main. Tintypes and photographs were the weapons with which Keogh and Clancy were at the time assaoilinh and hopeless shores. Outside the shop were set two large frames filed with specimens of their art and skill.

Keogh leaned in the doorway, his bold and humorous countenance wearing a look of interest at the unusual influx of life and sound into the street. When the meaning of the disturbance became clear to him he places a hand beside his mouth and shouted: "Hey! Frank!" in such a robustious voice that the feeble clamous of the natives was drowned and silenced.

Fifty yards away, on the seward side of the street, stood the adobe of the consul for the United States, Out from the door of this building tumbled Goodwin at the call. He had been smoking with Willard Geddie, the consul, on the back porch of the consulate, which was conceded to be the collest spot in Coralio.

"Hurry up," shouted Keagh. There's a riot in town on account of a telegram that's come for you. You want to be careful about these things, my boy. It won't do to trifle with the feelings of the public this way. You'll be getting a pink note some day with violet scent on it; and then the country'll be stepped in the throes of a revolution."
